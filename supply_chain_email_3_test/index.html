Supply Chains in the Time of COVID

PART 3: How Companies are Adjusting Their Strategies.

CEO of CampingWorld, Marcus Lemonis once said, "If you're not evolving, you're dying." These words ring particularly true for supply chains in the face of the COVID-19 pandemic and the hardships they've faced because of it. Fortunately, it seems many of the executives and decision-makers in charge of our global supply have taken these words to heart and are ready and willing to change and adopt new and exciting strategies to prevent future supply chain disruptions. Let's take a look at how some of these companies are, as Marcus Lemonis would say, "evolving."

Accounting giant Ernst and Young (EY) recently conducted a survey of 200 of the top supply chain executives to figure out how they're adjusting their strategies to deal with COVID disruptions. The results of this survey were interesting, but not surprising. Like most evolutions in business in the 21st Century, the answer seems to lie in tech integration. Most of the executives surveyed plan on increasing their companies reliance on technology to help alleviate some of the stresses and dispruptions caused by the pandemic. The end goal ? Well, 52% of those surveyed said they expect an autonomous or semi-autonomous supply chain by 2025. But what does this mean and what would this look like ?

For starters, they will be prioritizing supply chain visibility through the use of IoT devices and sensors to collect real time data on products throughout the entire product life-cycle. Information such as temperature readings and precise geographic location will enable micro-adjustments to supply chain strategy and management which will optimize shipping and lower costs. Also, through Aritificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning, innovations such as robots staffing warehouses, driverless forklifts, delivery drones, and automated planning will significantly reduce labor-related inefficiencies and human-error. These changes will also allow for near instantaneous responses to changes in customer demand.

Despite the challenges being faced by Supply Chains due to the pandemic - and they are numerous - companies and their executives are responding with innovative and tech-driven strategies to overcome these difficulties. And while the short term loss of revenue from these supply chain disruptions have been substantial, the evolution of this industry resulting from these challenges may prove to be the catalyst for extraordinary future growth and optimization. The pandemic was a dark and uncertain time, but the future of global supply chains appears to be bright.
